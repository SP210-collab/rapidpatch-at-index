What the data shows

1. Nearly half of public pothole reports go nowhere — and AT can't say why

Aucklanders filed 14,900 pothole reports by phone and online in 29 months. 6,813 of them — 45.7% — were closed "No Action Required": no repair recorded. When AT's own patrols spot a pothole, just 1.6% end that way. Some of the public's reports will be duplicates or holes below AT's intervention threshold — but nobody can say how many, because AT told us it does not record the reason a report is binned, declining that part of the request under s17(e) of LGOIMA. What doesn't get measured doesn't get fixed.

2. Rodney is a different planet

In the North Rural contract area — Rodney — 85.9% of public pothole reports were closed with no repair recorded. Rodney also logged the most pothole dispatches of any area (6,703) and is home to the region's most pothole-plagued road: Wayby Valley Road, with 186 dispatches — one every 4.7 days.

3. When AT does dispatch, it's genuinely fast

Credit where due: 96.1% of completed dispatches met AT's own response target. Median response on completed public reports runs 0.5–8 hours by area. The grievance in this data isn't speed — it's what never gets dispatched at all. (Response is not the same as a repair that lasts; this dataset doesn't measure durability.)

4. Central Auckland misses its own targets most

The Central area — Puketāpapa, Albert-Eden, Maungakiekie-Tāmaki, Ōrākei, Waitematā — hit its response target just 85.4% of the time, the worst in Auckland, with a 90th-percentile response of 3.5 days on public reports. It also consumed the most claims spend: $1.22M.

5. The bill is climbing

AT paid contractors at least $4.87M in itemised pothole claims over 29 months — and the run-rate rose 26% year-on-year ($1.80M in the 12 months to May 2025 → $2.28M in the 12 months to May 2026). That's an undercount: one contract area's pothole work is paid inside a monthly lump sum AT excluded from the data.

6. One emergency report in five: no action

Even among public reports AT itself classed as Emergency, 19.5% (398 of 2,038) were closed with no repair recorded.

Methodology & caveats

  • Source: Auckland Transport's response to LGOIMA request CAS-1344360-H7J1V4 (released 9 June 2026): Appendix A, sheet CT_DISPATCH — 26,863 pothole dispatch records, 1 January 2024 – 3 June 2026, with contract area, road, priority, call type, status, claim value and target vs actual response time per record.
  • "Public reports" = records with call type Call Centre (phone/online/app), 14,900 records. Patrol, contractor and council-logged jobs counted separately.
  • "Binned" / "no repair recorded" = call status No Action Required. This plausibly includes duplicates, reports on private land or non-AT roads, and holes below AT's intervention threshold (>100 mm wide or >50 mm deep, per AT's maintenance spec). AT holds no breakdown of reasons — it declined that part of the request under LGOIMA s17(e). We therefore report the rate, not a cause.
  • Response performance = actual vs target response time on completed dispatches, both as logged by AT. Response ≠ permanence of repair; this dataset says nothing about repairs that later failed.
  • Spend = sum of itemised claim values ($4.87M). South Rural's pothole repairs are paid in a monthly lump sum covering other work; AT supplied only that area's out-of-lump-sum claims, so the true total is higher.
  • Map positions: AT released road names, not coordinates. We geocoded the top 300 roads by dispatch count ( of all dispatches) via OpenStreetMap Nominatim; dots mark the road, not individual potholes. Roads that failed to geocode are omitted from the map but counted everywhere else.
  • Refresh cadence: annual LGOIMA re-request — this page is the first entry in the Pothole Report Card series.
  • Full dataset: available to journalists on request, as released by AT.
